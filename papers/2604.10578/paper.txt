Abstract. The growing demand for Embodied AI and VR applications
has highlighted the need for synthesizing high-quality 3D indoor scenes
from sparse inputs. However, existing approaches struggle to infer massive amounts of missing geometry in large unseen areas while maintaining global consistency, often producing locally plausible but globally inconsistent reconstructions. We present Rein3D, a framework that
reconstructs full 360° indoor environments by coupling explicit 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) with temporally coherent priors from video diffusion models. Our approach follows a "restore-and-refine" paradigm:
we employ a radial exploration strategy to render imperfect panoramic
videos along trajectories starting from the origin, effectively uncovering occluded regions from a coarse 3DGS initialization. These sequences
are restored by a panoramic video-to-video diffusion model and further
enhanced via video super-resolution to synthesize high-fidelity geometry and textures. Finally, these refined videos serve as pseudo-ground
truths to update the global 3D Gaussian field. To support this task,
we construct PanoV2V-15K, a dataset of over 15K paired clean and
degraded panoramic videos for diffusion-based scene restoration. Experiments demonstrate that Rein3D produces photorealistic and globally
consistent 3D scenes and significantly improves long-range camera exploration compared with existing baselines.
Keywords: 3D Indoor Scene Generation · Panoramic Video Diffusion
Models · 3D Gaussian Splatting

Related Work

2.1

3D Indoor Scene Generation

Powered by advances in differentiable rendering [19, 28] , 3D generative models
have expanded from isolated objects [46, 53] to complex scene synthesis. Early
iterative methods employ a “warp-and-refine” paradigm to extend the field of
view [6, 14, 26, 49] but often suffer from geometric drift over long trajectories. To
ensure global consistency, subsequent works utilize holistic 360-degree panoramas [32] or layouts [7] as unified priors, lifting them into 3D Gaussian Splatting
for real-time rendering [15, 43, 66]. However, these panorama-centric approaches
struggle with significant camera translations due to occlusion. Consequently, recent methods [16, 38, 62] use video diffusion models to hallucinate consistent
details during movement. In this work, we propose a novel framework that initializes a globally consistent scene from a panorama and leverages video diffusion
models to coherently restore and refine scene details during zoom-in exploration.
2.2

Panoramic Video Diffusion Models

The evolution of diffusion models [13, 36] revolutionized video synthesis, transitioning from UNet-based architectures with temporal layers [1, 12] to scalable Diffusion Transformers [31] that master complex spatio-temporal dynamics [4, 10, 20, 42, 58]. Although these foundation models excel at perspective content, applying them to 360-degree videos remains challenging due to spherical

distortion and boundary discontinuity. Pioneering efforts like 360DVD [44] and
VideoPanda [54] employ adapters or multi-view attention to handle spherical
geometry. Meanwhile, specific applications focus on animating static panoramas [22] or expanding narrow field-of-view videos to full immersive views [25,39].
Rencently, ViewPoint [8] introduces a novel map representation combined with
pano-perspective attention to ensure spatial continuity, whereas PanoWan [52]
employs latitude-aware sampling and rotated denoising to lift powerful DiT backbones to the panoramic domain. In this work, we propose a video-to-video framework that utilizes these advanced DiT priors for panoramic scene restoration.
By conditioning on preliminary renderings, our method effectively recovers highfidelity details in occluded regions while ensuring temporal consistency.

PanoV2V-15K Dataset Construction

3.1

Synthetic Panoramic Video Construction

To train and evaluate our video diffusion model for panoramic restoration, we
construct a large-scale panoramic video dataset follow previous work [7], the
dataset consisting of 15,050 distinct indoor scenes, covering diverse room types.
The dataset is synthesized from a curated repository of professionally designed
indoor environments, enabling high visual fidelity and broad scene diversity.
For each room, we first sample valid trajectories on the XY plane, extracting
anchor points every 0.5 m. To ensure stable and consistent camera movement,
we select the longest linear trajectory segment for each scene. We then place
a virtual omnidirectional camera at a fixed height of 1.6,m and record a 360◦
panoramic video at 10 FPS, moving from one end of this linear trajectory to the
other at a constant speed of 1 m/s. This procedure yields temporally coherent
supervision with smooth and physically plausible linear camera motion. The
data structure is shown in Fig. 2
Crucially, to construct the conditioning signal for training, we extract the
first panoramic view and its corresponding Ground Truth (GT) depth map at
the starting endpoint of the recorded trajectory. This single GT depth map is
used alongside the first panorama to initialize a coarse 3DGS representation
(detailed in Section 3.2). The rendered RGB and alpha (opacity) views from
this coarse 3DGS prior, combined with the first panoramic view, serve as the
conditioning input to guide the restoration of the subsequent panoramic video.
3.2

Coarse 3DGS Initialization

Building upon the conditioning signals extracted at the starting endpoint, we
utilize the first RGB panorama and its paired GT depth map to explicitly construct a coarse 3DGS geometric prior. Concretely, we lift the omnidirectional
RGB observation into a set of 3D Gaussian primitives by unprojecting each
equirectangular pixel into 3D space.

Specifically, we convert the GT depth map into metric distances via a fixed
scale factor, resizing it to match the panorama resolution if necessary. By computing the per-pixel viewing rays under the equirectangular projection model,
we back-project each pixel using its exact depth to obtain a 3D coordinate. This
3D point serves as the center position (mean) µi for a Gaussian primitive, which
is jointly initialized with its corresponding RGB color observation.
To ensure a stable and renderable 3D prior without requiring iterative optimization, we initialize the remaining attributes of the Gaussian primitives under a straightforward geometric assumption: each primitive is treated as a fully
opaque, isotropic sphere. Consequently, we assign identical values across all three
axes for the anisotropic scale si , set the rotation qi to a canonical identity quaternion, and fix the opacity αi to a constant value representing full opacity. This
deterministic initialization yields a lightweight yet effective coarse 3DGS scene,
providing reliable geometry-aware renderings — specifically, aligned RGB and
alpha panoramic sequences, to condition the downstream video diffusion model.

4.1

Preliminary

3D Gaussian Splatting. 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) [19, 68] represents a
scene as a set of K anisotropic 3D Gaussian primitives {Gk }K
k=1 . Each primitive
is defined by a 3D center µk ∈ R3 and a covariance matrix Σk ∈ R3×3 , which
together describe a continuous volumetric density:
  G_k(x)=\exp \!\left (-\frac {1}{2}(x-\mu _k)^{\top }\Sigma _k^{-1}(x-\mu _k)\right ). 

(1)

The covariance is parameterized by a rotation matrix Rk ∈ R3×3 and a diagonal
scale matrix Sk ∈ R3×3 ,
  \Sigma _k = R_k S_k S_k^{\top } R_k^{\top }. 
(2)
Given a camera viewpoint, visible primitives are ordered by depth and composited using front-to-back alpha blending. The rendered pixel color at image

Rein3D

7

location x is given by
  c(x)=\sum _{k=1}^{K} c_k(x)\,\alpha _k(x)\prod _{i=1}^{k-1}\bigl (1-\alpha _i(x)\bigr ), \label {eq:shading} 

(3)

where αk (x) denotes the view-dependent opacity of the k-th primitive at pixel
x, and ck (x) is its color, typically modeled using spherical harmonics to capture
view-dependent appearance [19]. This differentiable rasterization formulation enables efficient and stable optimization.
Given multi-view images with known camera parameters, 3DGS optimizes
primitive parameters {µk , Σk , αk , ck }K
k=1 by minimizing photometric reconstruction error across all training views, together with the intermittent density control.
In our setting, the supervision comes from a synthesized panoramic video, which
provides dense, temporally coherent observations with wide field-of-view coverage. Such panoramic views naturally reduce missing-view artifacts and improve
global visibility ordering, making them particularly well suited for reconstructing
a consistent 3D Gaussian representation.
Video Diffusion. Video diffusion models [2, 4, 5, 20, 42] extend diffusionbased generative modeling from images to videos by learning a distribution
over spatiotemporal signals. A video is represented as a sequence of frames
x ∈ RT ×H×W ×C . In practice, videos are often encoded into a latent space using
a pretrained autoencoder, yielding latent videos z ∈ RT ×H×W ×D .
Video diffusion defines a forward noising process that gradually perturbs the
clean video (or latent) z0 into Gaussian noise:
  q(\mathbf {z}_t \mid \mathbf {z}_0) = \mathcal {N}\!\left ( \mathbf {z}_t; \sqrt {\alpha _t}\mathbf {z}_0, (1-\alpha _t)\mathbf {I} \right ), 

(4)

where t ∈ {1, . . . , N } denotes the diffusion timestep and {αt } is a predefined
noise schedule. The corresponding reverse process is parameterized by a neural
network that iteratively denoises zt to recover z0 .
Video diffusion models can further be conditioned on auxiliary signals such
as text prompts, reference images, or preceding frames, enabling controllable and
temporally coherent video generation.
4.2

Initialization and Camera Exploration

Initial Scene Construction. To establish the base environment, we leverage
off-the-shelf models capable of handling both text and image inputs. Specifically, given a text prompt, we utilize DiT360 [9] to synthesize a high-resolution
panorama, whereas user-provided panoramic images can be directly adopted as
input. Subsequently, we employ DA2 [21] to estimate the corresponding depth
map. These RGB-D priors are then lifted into a preliminary 3D Gaussian Splatting representation, serving as the coarse initialization of the 3D scene.
Radial Trajectory Generation. To effectively uncover missing regions, we
employ a simple yet robust trajectory sampling strategy. Using the panoramic

8

D. Wang et al.

depth, we first calculate a safe navigation map. Rather than relying on small, localized camera movements around the origin, we formulate a radial search strategy consisting of τ uniformly distributed trajectories radiating outward from the
scene’s center. We dynamically identify the optimal global orientation for these
τ directions to maximize the cumulative movement range within the safe navigation area. We then render zoom-in RGB-Alpha panoramic videos along these
paths. These sequences, which naturally contain visual artifacts and transparent
holes, serve as the degraded inputs for our restoration network.
4.3

Panoramic Video Restoration Network

To transform imperfect RGB-Alpha renderings into high-fidelity videos, we design a specialized restoration network based on the Wan2.1-1.3B [42] DiT backbone. We adapt this foundation model to the panoramic domain through a conditional injection mechanism and specific spherical optimizations.
Architecture and Conditioning. Inspired by VACE [18], we utilize a context
adapter architecture to effectively handle multi-modal inputs. To ensure global
consistency during camera exploration, we explicitly incorporate a Context Anchor mechanism into the conditioning signal. Specifically, the input RGB-Alpha
video is processed to distinguish between observed content and missing regions.
Let V ∈ RT ×3×H×W denote the RGB video and M ∈ [0, 1]T ×1×H×W denote
the alpha channel. We decompose the input into two complementary streams:
  \mathbf {V}_{\text {background}} = \mathbf {V} \odot (\mathbf {1} - \mathbf {M}), \quad \mathbf {V}_{\text {foreground}} = \mathbf {V} \odot \mathbf {M} 

(5)

These streams are independently encoded by the pre-trained VAE encoder E
and concatenated along the channel dimension to form the video latent features
Zvideo :
  \mathbf {Z}_{\text {video}} = \text {Concat}(\mathcal {E}(\mathbf {V}_{\text {background}}), \mathcal {E}(\mathbf {V}_{\text {foreground}})) 
(6)
Crucially, since our trajectory begins from the scene origin, the starting panoramic
view I0 is fully observable (i.e., M0 = 1). We treat I0 as the Context Anchor,
encoding it to obtain Zanchor = E(I0 ). Finally, we construct the input condition
Zcondition by prepending the anchor to the video features along the temporal
dimension:
  \mathbf {Z}_{\text {condition}} = [\mathbf {Z}_{\text {anchor}}, \mathbf {Z}_{\text {video}}] 
(7)
This design ensures that the generation process is explicitly grounded in the
initial scene geometry, preventing temporal drift and color inconsistency.
Spherical Adaptation and Optimization. Standard diffusion models typically treat images as flat, uniform planes, ignoring the inherent geometric distortion of Equirectangular Projection (ERP). Specifically, pixels near the poles
are significantly stretched and over-sampled compared to the equator. To correct this, we introduce two complementary mechanisms that align both the noise
initialization and the training objective with spherical geometry:
(1) Latitude-Aware Sampling. Following PanoWan [52], we align the initial
noise with the geometric distortion of the input. Given an input resolution of

Rein3D

9

H × W , we define the latitude ϕ for a pixel at vertical coordinate y as ϕ =
(0.5 − y/H) · π. Since image content at high latitudes is horizontally stretched,
we apply a corresponding warp to the noise coordinates:
  x' = x_c + (x - x_c) \cdot \cos (\phi ). 

(8)

This operation effectively “stretches” the noise patterns at the poles, ensuring
that the noise density matches the visual signal when mapped to the 3D sphere.
(2) Latitude-Decay Loss. Complementary to the sampling, we adjust the
training objective to account for pixel redundancy. Since polar pixels correspond
to a much smaller physical area on the sphere, we reduce their influence on the
total loss. We define a spatial weight mask W (ϕ) as:
  W(\phi ) = \lambda + (1 - \lambda ) \cos (\phi ), 

(9)

where λ is a hyper-parameter. This formulation lowers the over-sampled polar
regions to match their actual geometric importance, while the constant λ ensures
that the model still maintains valid gradients to learn the structure at the poles.
4.4

3D Scene Refinement

To enhance visual quality of videos, we first employ FlashVSR [67] to upsample
the video sequences, effectively recovering high-frequency details. These highresolution panoramic frames are then projected into perspective views to serve
as pseudo-ground truths. Finally, we fine-tune the 3D Gaussian Splatting against
these views. During this optimization, we incorporate robust densification strategies and anti-aliasing techniques to effectively suppress geometric artifacts and
further refine the scene texture.

Experiments

5.1

Implementation Details

We implement our Rein3D framework using PyTorch. The video restoration
network is initialized from the pre-trained VACE-1.3B [18] checkpoint. We finetune the model on our proposed dataset using the AdamW [24] optimizer with a
constant learning rate of 5 × 10−5 . During training, the input panoramic video
sequences are fixed to a length of 41 frames, with a spatial resolution of 448×896.
The total batch size is set to 32, and the training proceeds for 5,000 steps on 4
NVIDIA H200 (141GB) GPUs.
For the final 3D scene refinement stage, we utilize the efficient gsplat library [59]. We optimize the 3D Gaussian Splatting for 15,000 steps against the
upsampled pseudo-ground truths. To ensure high rendering quality, we enable
anti-aliasing [63] during rasterization to effectively suppress geometric artifacts.

projections. Discarding this decay mechanism (λ = 1) forces the model to waste
capacity on heavily distorted and geometrically redundant pixels, resulting in
inferior spherical consistency with a WS-PSNR of 22.97. Conversely, setting
λ = 0 entirely ignores the poles, which also harms overall fidelity. While moderate
values like λ = 0.5 slightly inflate the standard PSNR to 23.80, they compromise
temporal stability. We found that our default setting of λ = 0.1 achieves the
optimal balance, delivering the highest spherical reconstruction quality with a
WS-PSNR of 23.45 and WS-SSIM of 0.820, alongside the most stable temporal
consistency with an FVD of 5.26.

6

Conclusion

In this paper, we introduced Rein3D, a novel framework for high-fidelity 3D
indoor scene generation that synergizes the explicit geometry of 3D Gaussian
Splatting with the generative consistency of video diffusion models. By formulating scene completion as a panoramic video restoration task, we effectively resolve
severe occlusions and geometric ambiguities inherent in single-view reconstruction. To bridge the data gap, we constructed a large-scale dataset specifically
tailored for panoramic video inpainting, enabling robust learning of indoor scene
priors. Experimental results demonstrate that our cyclic "restore-refine" strategy

produces photorealistic, 360-degree consistent representations that significantly
outperform state-of-the-art baselines, particularly in supporting long-range camera exploration. Future work will focus on supporting detailed semantic editing
and dynamic environment synthesis. We believe this work serves as a solid step
towards scalable, high-quality 3D content creation for Virtual Reality and Embodied AI applications.
